In November 2008, the same election that made Barack Obama president, California voters did something surprising. Obama was a candidate who called for change and the expansion of civil rights, but voters opted for a change: to take away a right.

By 52% to 48%, Californians passed Proposition 8, which amended the state constitution to ban same-sex marriage. Roughly 18,000 same-sex couples had already gotten married, and Prop 8 ended that overnight.

However, 2 years later, federal judge Vaughn Walker ruled Prop 8 unconstitutional. The result of a direct democratic vote was undone.

Proposition 8 was direct democracy at its purest. Anyone eligible to vote in California could cast their ballot. With both sides spending millions on campaigning for and against Prop 8, the result was clear: a majority of Californians said no to same-sex marriage.

In 2010, federal Judge Vaughn Walker ruled that Prop 8 was unconstitutional. He didn't claim that the voters made a mistake in their judgement, but instead said that they did not have the right to make that decision in the first place. He ruled that the 14th Amendment protected equal treatment under the law, and a majority vote could not deny equal rights to the minority, no matter how many voted.

Defenders of Prop 8

They had won the vote and underwent due process to secure the Proposition. Direct democracy was supposed to be the most democratic process there is. Citizens decided directly. Now an unelected judge had erased their victory with a single ruling.

Defenders of the ruling

A major point of the Constitution is that some things weren't up to a vote. You can't vote to bring back slavery. You can't vote to ban religion. You can't vote to abolish voting. Some rights exist above majority rule, and the Constitution is a safeguard that makes democracy work.

The result was a country where same-sex marriage is a recognized constitutional right. The process was a federal court overruling 7 million voters in California's highest-turnout election.

So which one was more democratic — the vote, or the ruling?

Section 02 — The Facts

What's Established, What Isn't

Confirmed
  • Proposition 8 passed in California in November 2008 by a margin of 52.24% to 47.76%, with over 13 million votes cast.
  • Federal Judge Vaughn Walker ruled in Perry v. Schwarzenegger (2010) that Prop 8 violated the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection and Due Process clauses.
  • The U.S. Supreme Court declined to rule on the merits of the case in 2013, allowing the lower court ruling to stand.
Contested
  • Whether the 14th Amendment's drafters in 1868 intended its protections to apply to same-sex marriage.

Section 03 — The Framework

The Tensions Underneath

This lesson sits on one main tension, with a second underneath it.

Main Tension

Majority Rule vs. Minority Rights

When do numbers decide, and when don't they?

The majority-rule view

Democracy means the people decide. If 52% of Californians vote for something, that's a legitimate process and should lead to a legitimate outcome. The point of voting is that we accept results even when they aren't personally favorable.

The minority-rights view

Democracy without limits fails to be democracy — it becomes mob rule. Alexis de Tocqueville's "tyranny of the majority" names this exact worry. A vote to deny rights to a minority isn't a legitimate democratic outcome; it's a failure that democracy must overcome. Courts exist to protect against these failures.

Underneath

Process vs. Outcome

Should we judge a decision by how it was made, or what it produced?

If someone thinks Prop 8 was wrong because it produced an unjust result, they're prioritizing outcome. If someone thinks the court ruling was wrong because it bypassed a legitimate vote, they're prioritizing process. People often switch between these based on whether they like the result — which is exactly why the framework matters.

Ask yourself: What's your principle when the process produces an outcome you disagree with?

Section 04 — The Stakes

What You're Actually Choosing

The question now isn't whether same-sex marriage should be legal. That fight is over. The question underneath is harder: when a majority and a court disagree, who should we trust more?

If you trust the majority

You have to be willing to accept a majority decision you disagree with.

If you trust the court

You have to accept that some unelected judges can overturn votes you helped win.
