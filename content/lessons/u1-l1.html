You probably just spent some time learning about the fundamentals of Democracy and the groundwork of the United States' government, and hopefully, Democracy as a whole seems like a good idea to you. In concept, it is a pretty great thing: natural rights, a social contract, popular sovereignty, and limited government, a structure set up so that the people may decide their own government.

But, here is a simple truth: Democracy isn't located in the capital of the USA. It isn't the Constitution established by the founding fathers. It isn't a system that runs in the background while we live out our normal lives. Democracy is something we do.

To argue, to read carefully, to take positions, to vote, these are all things that you can participate in to contribute to democracy. The problem is, when people don't do these anymore, it becomes something else.

Section 01 — The Data

What the Numbers Say

A metric we can look at to see if people are participating in the democratic system is voter turn-out rates. Hover over any bar to see the details.

In the past 20+ years, voter turnout has never surpassed 65%, dipping as low as 36.7% in 2014.

To be clear, the people who don't cast their ballot are not bad people. They're busy, tired, and have been told their whole lives that politics is corrupt, and that ultimately their vote doesn't matter. What does this result in for these people? They tune out, scroll past political headlines, and won't engage in any conversation with family members when the topic is brought up. They might tell themselves, "I'll engage when something important happens." But when something inevitably happens, they realize that they don't know enough to form an opinion, so they do nothing.

Section 02 — The Chain Reaction

How Democracies Quietly Fail

When the majority begins to think this way, the consequences don't show up all at once. They unfold in stages. Click through to see what actually happens.

01
Political conversations disappear.

People stop debating, stop questioning, stop having to defend their views. At first, this seems like a relief — fewer arguments, less hostility.

02
Tribalism takes over.

Without the friction of real debate, opinions get formed not through critical thinking, but through allegiance. People believe what their party believes, because that's their team.

03
Nuance dies.

Conversations become finger-pointing and trash-talking. Nobody is persuadable. Nobody is listening. Disagreement isn't a starting point anymore — it's a verdict.

04
The loudest voices run the country.

When most people are checked out, the ones with the most funding and the biggest megaphones drive the direction of everything. The institutions still exist — but they're being steered by a small fraction of engaged people.

This is how democracies fail.

You are about to enter into that world.

Section 03 — What's Coming for You

The World You're About to Vote In

If you are taking a government class right now, that means you are probably a senior in high school, and in a full year, you will be a legal adult. This means you will be able to form your own political identity, and vote for who you want to vote for.

But be weary. Here's what's waiting for you:

📱 Political ads engineered to make you angry.
🎯 TikToks, Reels, and Shorts that pull you into snap judgments before you've thought twice.
📰 News headlines that harden your opinion before you even get a chance to think about it.
Recognize any of these?
If you nodded at even one of those, you're already inside the system this lesson is about. The goal is to learn to spot it, slow down, and think anyway.

If the information around you is designed to manipulate and shape your opinion before you've even had a chance to think, the question becomes:

How do you form your own opinions?

That is what the following lessons are for. You'll work through real political questions — surveillance after 9/11, vaccine mandates, the filibuster, even your own social media feed. None of them have clean answers, but they all involve real tradeoffs between real outcomes.

All of them will ask you the same question in a different form: What do you actually think, and why?

You will learn that political disagreements are not usually about facts, but about values. What do people actually prioritize when two good things conflict?

Section 04 — The Toolkit

The 8 Frameworks Underneath the Politics

Almost every political argument you'll ever encounter maps onto one of these tensions. Click any framework to see the question it's really asking.

⚖️Liberty vs. Equality
Should government maximize freedom, or fairness?
🛡️Liberty vs. Order
How much freedom do we trade for safety and stability?
🗳️Majority Rule vs. Minority Rights
When do numbers decide, and when don't they?
👤Individual vs. Community
Is the basic unit of politics the person or the group?
🏛️Centralized vs. Decentralized Power
Who decides, and at what level?
📋Process vs. Outcomes
Does fairness mean fair rules, or fair results?
🔄Tradition vs. Progress
When do we keep what works, and when do we change it?
💡Idealism vs. Pragmatism
Should politics pursue what's right, or what's possible?

Once you see the framework underneath the politics, you can stop and evaluate the things happening around you. You can become someone who can think about it, push back, and vote. You will become harder to manipulate.

But here's the thing: democracy isn't just about one person. It is about being able to debate and talk through difficult things. In a democracy, people need to engage. That is why at the end of every topic, there will be a time for you to take a position and to defend your thoughts. So you can learn to have discourse with those around you.

That's the project, and that's what these lessons are for.

Lesson Summary

  • Democracy is something people do, not something that happens on its own. When people stop participating, there is no longer democracy.
  • When most people stop, tribalism results: opinions formed by political loyalty rather than critical thinking. This is when the loudest and wealthiest voices run the country.
  • Most political disagreements are about values, not facts. There are 8 major frameworks that shape almost every political argument.
  • The goal of these lessons is not to tell you what to think, but make sure you can think clearly, honestly, and with evidence, and then engage.
